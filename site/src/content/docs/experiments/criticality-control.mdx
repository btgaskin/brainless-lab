**Question.** The [Falandays](/nodes/falandays/) homeostatic rule holds each reservoir's branching
ratio $m$ near $1$ — the textbook signature of self-organized criticality. This page asks the causal
question directly and end-to-end: is $m$ the *controlled* variable, is it an independently *tunable*
knob, and is function driven by proximity to $m \approx 1$? Following the arc from the node in
isolation, to the [Oosawa](/nodes/falandays/#the-oosawa-drive-a-variant-for-contrast) drive we
actually run, to the 48-agent [forage](/experiments/social-foraging/) swarm, the tested
settings show a consistent pattern—but not an exhaustive impossibility result. At the node,
$m \approx 1$ is an **ambiguous byproduct** of
rate homeostasis. Under the Oosawa drive, threshold-gap-modulated membrane noise changes the
operating regime. Across the tested foraging settings, every functioning cell had
$m\approx1$, while the only observed sub-$0.9$ cell was nearly silent. These exploratory
probes therefore did not separate criticality from function; they do not show that such a
separation is impossible.

**Design.** Six probes along that arc: (A) the target-mechanism sweep on the single-agent `:wall`
base — the node-level *ambiguity* result; (B) tracking, `base` vs the Oosawa drive — where the
easy-base "freeze the target for free" result **reverses**; (C) a single-agent forage probe;
(D) a `noise_gain` sweep across contexts that characterizes the threshold-gap drive; (E) the
48-agent transmission config, asking whether $m$ tracks foraging performance; and (F) a bounded
attempt to *move* $m$ on the swarm, reporting the range reached in the tested grid. A short collective null-test (the flat
swarm $m$ against a circular-shift surrogate) is kept as a supporting measurement caveat.

:::note[The honest posture]
This is a *dissection*, not a leaderboard. We are not asking how high $m$ climbs or how good the
score gets—we are asking **which actuator regulates what**, and whether the branching ratio near
$1$ is a tuned target, a driver of function, or neither. In the tested settings it is consistent
with a **defended-byproduct hypothesis** and was not separated from function. Where a claim is a
spot-check rather than a promoted study, it is flagged as such — and one earlier phrasing, "$m$ **decoupled** from
performance," is **retired** here (see [the honest conclusion](#the-honest-conclusion)).
:::

## The rule — two actuators, one error

Each node runs the **allostatic** Falandays reservoir (full update and learning equations in
[Nodes → Falandays](/nodes/falandays/); we do not re-derive them here). The error is the post-spike
distance from set-point, $e = \text{acts} - \text{target}$, and **two homeostatic actuators share
that one error**:

- **The weight rule.** Eligible weights — those whose presynaptic node fired last tick — move
  *opposite* the error, $w \mathrel{-}= (e / \text{counts}) \cdot \texttt{lrate\_wmat}$. This is the
  fast, per-synapse corrector.
- **The target rule.** The set-point itself drifts *toward* activity,
  $\text{target} \mathrel{+}= e \cdot \texttt{lrate\_targ}$, and is **floored** at `targ_min`. The
  spike threshold is $\text{target} \cdot \texttt{threshold\_mult}$.

So the target is an **allostatic** (moving, floored) set-point, not a fixed one. The `targ_min` floor
matters below: when activity runs *below* set-point for long stretches, the error is persistently
negative, the target drifts *down*, and it **clamps** onto `targ_min` — turning the allostatic
set-point into an effectively *fixed* one. Whether the floor is live or inert becomes a clean readout
of whether the reservoir is under-fed.

## Experiment A — node-level ambiguity on the wall base

**On the wall base, $m \approx 1$ is ambiguous — it reads the same at the healthy plateau, at
saturation, and at silent collapse — so this estimate alone cannot identify whether $m$ is a
controlled variable.** Substrate `:wall`,
`node=:falandays_base`, single agent, **10 seeds, $T = 2000$**. We measure three things at once:
**nav** = normalized wall score (function — note it *ceilings at 1.0*, because wall is easy, so the
**failures** are the signal, not the ceiling), **rate** = mean firing rate (activity), and **$m$** =
pooled MR (multistep-regression) branching ratio (criticality). One knob moves at a time from the
default; `both` = freeze target *and* weights.

Four findings, each read straight off the table:

- **(i) Function is a plateau, and it breaks only at rate-regime failures.** nav sits at $\approx 1$
  across the *entire* healthy band — $m$ anywhere from $0.92$ to $0.99$, rate anywhere from $0.29$ to
  $0.79$. It collapses in exactly two places, both **rate** failures: **saturation** (`threshold_mult`
  $< 1.75 \Rightarrow$ rate $\to 1$, nav $\to 0.52$) and **silent collapse** (`lrate_targ` $\ge 0.1$
  or `lrate_wmat` $= 0.1 \Rightarrow$ rate $\to 0$, nav $\to 0$). The healthy plateau spans a wide
  band of $m$; the failures are read off the rate.
- **(ii) The weight rule is the load-bearing homeostat — on *this* base.** *Freezing the target*
  entirely (`lrate_targ` $= 0$) leaves function **perfect** (nav $1.000$, and $m$ actually *higher*
  at $0.990$), while *weakening or killing the weight rule* saturates and dies. **But read the
  "freeze the target for free" result as wall-base-specific.** On the Oosawa node we actually run
  (next section), freezing the target instead **halves** function — the easy input-driven base is the
  special case, not the rule. The weight rule still has a **Goldilocks strength** ($0.5$–$1.0$ works,
  $2.0$ over-corrects to nav $0.730$), and killing it always kills the network.
- **(iii) The target sets the operating rate, and has a hard stability limit.** `targ_min` and
  `threshold_mult` slide the rate/regime smoothly ($m$ tracking as a byproduct — e.g. `targ_min`
  $0.1 \to 4.0$ walks rate $0.29 \to 0.39$ and $m$ $0.976 \to 0.923$). But driving the set-point
  *too fast* — `lrate_targ` $\ge 0.1$ — **destabilizes the loop into collapse** (rate $\to 0$, nav
  $\to 0$). The target is a slow governor, not a fast corrector.
- **(iv) The estimated $m$ is non-discriminative — and this is the headline.** It sits between $0.92$ and
  $0.99$ whenever the controller works *at all*, and it is **ambiguous**:
  $m \approx 0.99$ shows up at the healthy plateau (`lrate_targ` $= 0$), at full saturation
  (`threshold_mult` $= 0.5$, $m = 0.991$, broken), and at dead silence (`threshold_mult` $= 3.0$,
  $m = 0.995$, nav $0.000$) alike. A number that is the *same* at the working point, at saturation,
  and at silence cannot by itself identify a control target.

![m vs firing rate, colored by function — m≈1 appears at the healthy plateau, at saturation, and at silent collapse](/experiments/criticality-control/m-rate-plane.png)

*Every Experiment-A config on the (rate, $m$) plane, coloured by function. **$m \approx 0.95$–$1.0$
appears in three places** — the healthy plateau (green, rate $\approx 0.3$–$0.8$), saturation (pale,
rate $\approx 1$, broken), and silent collapse (red, rate $\approx 0$). The branching ratio alone
cannot tell function from failure; the **rate** (x-axis) does. Function lives in a rate window, not
at a criticality value — and "$m \approx 1$" is the signature of saturation and silence just as much
as of the working regime.*

This is the single-agent version of a caveat that shadows [Bessone &
Plantec's](/notes/emergent-macro-criticality/) effective branching ratio: their $\sigma_\text{eff}$
reaches $1$ in the **saturated** phase as readily as at the critical band, so $\sigma_\text{eff} \to
1$ alone cannot certify criticality — the rate (or the avalanche-shape exponents) has to
disambiguate. Our figure draws that ambiguity explicitly.

## The node we use — tracking, base vs the Oosawa drive

**The Oosawa drive runs the reservoir hotter and *less* critical than the base, and it reverses
Experiment A's "freeze the target for free" — under the drive, the target adaptation is
load-bearing.** The forage swarm and every live demo run `:falandays_oosawa`, not the plain base, so
the node we actually use gets its own dissection. Substrate `:tracking`, single agent, **6 seeds,
$T = 3000$**, default parameters:

| tracking, default | m | rate | mean target | % at floor |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| base | 0.986 | 0.359 | 1.037 | 26% |
| oosawa | 0.945 | 0.822 | 2.125 | ~1% |

Four findings:

- **(a) The drive runs it hotter and *less* critical.** The Oosawa term nearly doubles the firing
  rate ($0.36 \to 0.82$) and pushes $m$ *down*, away from $1$ ($0.986 \to 0.945$). So "the node we
  use" is the *less* critical of the two — the drive trades a little branching-ratio proximity for a
  much livelier reservoir.
- **(b) The floor mechanism is drive-dependent — and it is the tell.** The base is low-activity, so
  its error runs persistently negative, the target drifts down, and it **sinks onto the floor**: 26%
  of node-targets are clamped at `targ_min`, i.e. the "allostatic" set-point is behaving like a
  *fixed* one. The Oosawa drive pushes activity up, so the target **floats free to $\approx 2.1$**,
  with only $\sim 1\%$ clamped — the set-point is genuinely allostatic and the floor is **inert**. The
  floor is a starvation indicator: live when the reservoir is under-fed, dead when the drive feeds it.
- **(c) CORRECTION to Experiment A.** On the Oosawa node, **freezing the target halves function**
  (frac30 $0.302 \to 0.158$). The target adaptation *is* load-bearing under the drive — the exact
  *opposite* of the wall-base result, where freezing the target cost nothing. The Experiment-A
  finding was true of the easy, input-driven base; it does **not** carry to the node we run.
- **(d) $m$ still does not track function.** Across the drive's operating range, frac30 swings widely
  ($0.05$–$0.52$) while $m$ barely moves. The ambiguity of Experiment A reappears on the node we use:
  function moves, $m$ does not.

## Single-agent foraging probe

**On the task we actually run, the same picture holds: freezing the target hurts *and* pushes $m$
back up toward the base pin, and the floor is inert whenever the swarm is fed.** Single-agent forage,
`node=:falandays_oosawa`, **6 seeds**:

| single-agent forage, oosawa | forage | m | note |
|---|---|---|---|
| default | 0.730 | 0.934 | target floats free |
| freeze_target (`lrate_targ=0`) | 0.578 | 0.984 | freezing **hurts** *and* pushes $m$ **up** toward the base pin |

Freezing the target is not free here either — it costs $\approx 0.15$ of forage score, and it moves
$m$ the "wrong" way, *up* toward the base's $\approx 0.99$ rather than the drive's lower value. Two
floor probes complete the picture: `targ_min=0.5` leaves function unchanged ($0.727$) with the target
floating up to $\approx 2.8$ (floor **inert**), while `targ_min=4.0` puts the floor above the
reservoir's reach and the network goes **silent**. The floor only bites when it is set high enough to
starve a network the drive would otherwise feed — consistent with the tracking read, and with the
[social-foraging](/experiments/social-foraging/) tuning where the Oosawa `noise_gain` sweet spot is
$0.2$–$0.3$.

## The threshold-gap drive — how noise moves criticality

**The implemented Oosawa term scales Gaussian membrane noise with the positive gap to firing
threshold. It is generally active below that threshold, including at the homeostatic target when
`threshold_mult > 1`.** The probes below show context-dependent associations between its gain,
rate, and $m$; they do not establish a physiological starvation switch. With the drive gain at
zero, the Oosawa preset reduces to the base update:

- **`oosawa` with `noise_gain=0` matches `base` in the tested forage runs.** Forage / rate /
  $m$: blind $0.550 / 0.247 / 0.993$; sighted $0.552 / 0.309 / 0.991$ — 4 seeds. At
  `noise_gain=0`, the state-dependent drive contributes nothing.
- **Tracking (input-poor): noise *moves* $m$.** As `noise_gain` rises $0 \to 0.4$, $m$ climbs from
  $\approx 0.62$ (noise $0$, genuinely **subcritical**) to $\approx 0.98$, and rate $0.17 \to 0.81$.
  Here the drive **is** the activity source, so it is what carries the reservoir up to $m \approx 1$.
  *(Honesty flag: the clean $0.62 \to 0.98$ range is a **single-seed** read. A 5-seed run gave a
  narrower $0.89 \to 0.98$ and a noisy frac30 — the direction is solid, the exact bottom of the range
  is not.)*
- **Forage swarm: the tested gain did not move $m$.** Across `noise_gain` $0 \to 0.8$, $m$ stays
  pinned $\approx 0.98$–$0.99$ in both blind and sighted conditions; the drive only bumps the *rate*
  at the top of the range. This may reflect task input and the adaptive target, but the current
  observations do not record when the drive amplitude is zero or establish why the contexts differ.
- **Sighted vs blind raises the operating point, not the criticality.** Turning on conspecific vision
  floats the target up ($1.49 \to 3.27$) and lifts the rate, but $m$ stays $\approx 0.99$. Social
  input is *more feed*, not *more criticality*.

**Working hypothesis.** In these settings, task input, threshold-gap noise, and the adaptive target
jointly place functioning reservoirs in a regime where $m$ remains near $1$. The same rate-pinning
pattern appeared in the [tracking sweep](/experiments/tracking-param-sweep/). Testing the mechanism
requires recording drive amplitude directly and using a frozen intervention that changes $m$
without simultaneously changing rate or task input.

## Does it correspond to foraging performance?

**On the 48-agent transmission config, foraging performance and transmission vary enormously while
$m$ stays flat — but flatness alone proves nothing.** Canonical social-forage config (48 agents,
`node=:falandays_oosawa`; see [social-foraging](/experiments/social-foraging/)), `noise_gain` swept:

| noise_gain | social forage | ΔΔ (transmission) |
|---|---|---|
| 0.0 | 0.692 | **+0.098** |
| 0.8 | 0.490 | **−0.010** |

As `noise_gain` rises $0 \to 0.8$, social forage falls $0.692 \to 0.490$ and the transmission
advantage $\Delta\Delta$ collapses from $+0.098$ to $-0.010$ — a large functional swing — while
$m_\text{node}$ stays **pinned $0.989$–$0.996$** across the *entire* sweep.

:::caution[Flat $m$ cannot establish "decoupling"]
It is tempting to read "performance moves, $m$ doesn't" as *criticality is irrelevant to function*.
It is not. **If $m$ never varies, its effect on function was never tested.** A flat $m$ beside a
varying performance shows only that the **noise** acts on the behaviour — it says nothing about
whether criticality, *had it varied*, would have mattered. To make any causal claim we must actually
**move $m$**. The next section reports the range reached in this exploratory grid.
:::

## Trying to vary m in the tested grid

**Within these two exploratory sweeps, attempts to push the 48-agent swarm off $m \approx 1$ either
did not move $m$ appreciably or produced a nearly silent reservoir. No functional subcritical cell
was observed in this grid.** Two sweeps on the canonical 48-agent config:

- **Target/floor sweep.** Across every tested target and `targ_min` setting — functional and dead
  alike — $m$ remained at **$0.980$–$0.995$**. This is consistent with weight-mediated
  stabilization, but the sweep does not establish that mechanism or show that target knobs cannot
  move $m$ outside the tested range.
- **Feed sweep (`input_weight`, `link_p`).** The *only* configuration with $m$ below $0.9$ was
  `input_weight=0.25`, which gave $m = 0.867$ **but rate $= 0.001$ (nearly silent)** and social forage
  $\approx$ the $0.456$ null — not a working sub-critical reservoir. Every configuration
  that actually forages sat at $m = 0.977$–$0.993$. Structural change (`link_p`) **barely moved $m$**
  in this grid, again consistent with—but not proof of—weight-mediated stabilization.

![Foraging performance vs reservoir m — every functional swarm sits at m≈0.98–0.99; the only sub-0.9 point is silent](/experiments/criticality-control/forage-confound.png)

*Every tested 48-agent config from the noise, target/floor, and feed sweeps, plotted as foraging performance
vs reservoir $m$, coloured by firing rate. Every swarm that actually forages (social well above the
$0.456$ null) sits at **$m \approx 0.98$–$0.99$**; the single sub-$0.9$ point is a **silenced**
reservoir (rate $\approx 0$, performance at null). This grid contains no
functional-subcritical comparison.*

## The bounded conclusion

**In the tested settings, criticality and function remain confounded, so these probes do not answer
the causal question.** The observations are consistent with—but do not establish—the hypothesis
that weight adaptation defends an $m\approx1$ regime across some input and structural
perturbations. At the node, $m\approx1$ is ambiguous because similar values occur at health,
saturation, and silence. In the tested swarm grid, functioning cells clustered around
$m=0.98$–$0.99$, and the only observed sub-$0.9$ point was nearly silent.

We therefore **retire the word "decoupled."** An earlier framing called $m$ "decoupled from
performance." But a flat, pinned $m$ **cannot demonstrate decoupling** — if $m$ never varies, its
effect on function was never tested. The honest relation is **not separated here**: criticality
and function are confounded in this grid. What survives is narrow: $m\approx1$ is an observed
correlate of functioning reservoirs in these settings, not a demonstrated tuned target or
independently set cause. A fixed-weight comparison or direct intervention on branching dynamics is
needed before making a stronger invariance claim.

This **refines, and does not contradict,** the [social-foraging](/experiments/social-foraging/)
page's "function without criticality." These probes did not find an independent criticality dial in
the tested parameter region.

## The collective null — a supporting measurement caveat

**Subordinate to the arc above: the swarm's flat $m \approx 0.97$ does not even survive a null, so it
was never a clean collective-criticality signal to begin with.** Substrate `:forage`,
`node=:falandays_oosawa`, 48 agents, social, $T = 3000$. Here $m_\text{agent}$ is the MR branching
ratio on the `:align` collective activity; **activity** = $\overline{\text{agent\_activity}}/N$ (a
value $\to 1$ would be saturation); and the **crossshift null** is a circular-shift surrogate that
destroys inter-agent timing while preserving each agent's own statistics — so its ratio $=
\text{real}/\text{null\_mean} \approx 1$ means *no real cross-agent structure*. Representative cells
across conspecific-gain (cg) and vision-range (vr):

| cg | vr | m_agent | activity | null real/mean → ratio |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1.5 | 0.977 | 0.122 | 0.982 / 0.921 → **1.07** |
| 4 | 4.0 | 0.977 | 0.150 | 0.977 / 0.943 → **1.04** |
| 4 | 6.0 | 0.969 | 0.150 | 0.977 / 0.955 → **1.02** |
| 16 | 4.0 | 0.963 | 0.150 | 0.969 / 0.958 → **1.01** |
| 2 | 2.5 | 0.954 | 0.149 | 0.975 / 0.923 → **1.06** |

Three observations narrow the interpretation of a flat $m \approx 1$ in this small spot-check:

- **$m_\text{agent}$ is flat $\approx 0.95$–$0.98$** across the cells — consistent with an
  allostatic rate-stabilization hypothesis and with the narrow range the
  [tracking sweep](/experiments/tracking-param-sweep/) reported.
- **Activity is only $\approx 0.12$–$0.15$ — nowhere near saturation.** So the flat $m$ is *not* the
  saturation artifact (a network firing flat-out also reads $m \to 1$); this swarm fires sparsely and
  still shows $m \approx 0.97$.
- **The crossshift-null ratio is $\approx 1.0$** ($1.01$–$1.07$). $m_\text{agent}$ barely exceeds a
  surrogate whose inter-agent timing has been shuffled away — so essentially *all* of the branching
  signal survives destroying the cross-agent structure. In this spot-check, the $m \approx 0.97$
  estimate is therefore indistinguishable from a predominantly within-agent statistic; it does not
  establish a collective critical avalanche.

:::note[This is a spot-check, and a caveat — not a clean claim]
Experiment D is **5 representative cells**, with **3 seeds** for $m$/activity and **1 seed $\times$
30 shifts** for each null. Read it as a **measurement caveat** — the flat collective $m$ fails its
own null, so there is no clean collective-criticality claim for function to be "displaced from" —
subordinate to the main arc, not an independent result. It should be scaled (20+ seeds, the whole
cg $\times$ vr landscape) before promotion to a run-dir study.
:::

## A cleaner proposed test — a fixed-weight, evolved reservoir (future work)

**One clean proposed way to test criticality's causal role is a *different node family, evolved*,
rather than another tuning sweep of this one.** To ask whether criticality *causes* good function,
the design should independently manipulate a criticality-relevant quantity while function is
optimised. The tested sweeps did not expose such an independent dial. A suitable candidate is a
**fixed-weight reservoir** — the
compartmental / CTRNN nodes — where $m$ is **set by the spectral radius $\rho(W)$ of a *static* weight
matrix**, with **no homeostat defending it**, and the reservoir is **evolved** for the task
*separately at each $m$ level*. That design would make the manipulated network regime more
independent of the task optimization and improve causal identifiability.

Per [Larremore, Shew & Restrepo](/notes/spectral-radius-criticality/), the right control quantity for
that sweep is the topology-general critical point $\lambda(W) = 1$, of which the branching criterion
$\sigma = 1$ (what our node estimator targets) is only the *homogeneous* special case. So the
fixed-weight test should be run over $\lambda(W)$ directly, not over the rate-driven branching
statistic the homeostat pins. That is a node-family change **plus** evolution — the open route this
page deliberately cannot close by tuning.

## Reproduce it

**Experiment A (node).** One cell per row of the table — sweep a single knob, read function,
activity, and criticality off the same run:

```julia
using BrainlessLab, Statistics

s = 1
sim = simulate(:wall; node=:falandays_base,
    lrate_targ=0.0, targ_min=1.0, threshold_mult=2.0, lrate_wmat=1.0,  # move one at a time
    ticks=2000, seed=s)

nav  = sim.metrics.nav_score                              # function (ceilings at 1.0 on :wall)
rate = sim.metrics.rate_mean                              # activity
m    = branching_ratio_mr(sim; level=:pooled).m_mr        # criticality (byproduct)
```

The table rows are the mean over 10 seeds; `both` freezes target *and* weights
(`lrate_targ=0.0, lrate_wmat=0.0`).

**The Oosawa node (tracking / forage).** Same three reads on the node we actually use, plus the
`:targets` channel for the **floor** readout (mean target, and the fraction clamped at `targ_min`):

```julia
using BrainlessLab, Statistics

s = 1
sim = simulate(:tracking; node=:falandays_oosawa,
    noise_gain=0.8, targ_min=1.0,           # noise_gain=0 ⇒ byte-identical to :falandays_base
    record=(:rate, :targets), ticks=3000, seed=s)   # :rate is needed by the branching estimator

frac30 = sim.metrics.frac_within_30deg                    # function (tracking); forage_score on :forage
rate   = sim.metrics.rate_mean
m      = branching_ratio_mr(sim; level=:pooled).m_mr      # reservoir MR estimate (:node for a swarm)

# floor readout: mean set-point and % of node-targets clamped onto targ_min
targ   = getchannel(sim.recorder, :targets)               # per-tick target vectors
last   = targ[end]
nodes  = (last[1] isa AbstractVector) ? last[1] : last    # unwrap the single-agent frame
mean_target  = mean(nodes)
pct_at_floor = 100 * mean(abs.(nodes .- 1.0) .< 1e-6)     # targ_min = 1.0 here
```

Swap `:tracking` for the 48-agent `:forage` config (read `sim.metrics.forage_score`, and
`branching_ratio_mr(sim; level=:agent, observable=(kind=:align, neighbor_radius=vr)).m_mr` for the
swarm) to reproduce the foraging and confound sweeps.

**Experiment D (collective null).** Run the social forage config, then measure the swarm branching
ratio and its circular-shift null on the same rollout:

```julia
using BrainlessLab, Statistics, Random
```

## Open questions

:::note[The honest posture]
None of this says criticality is irrelevant *in general*. In **this designed homeostatic node**, the
observed branching-ratio estimate near $1$ is consistent with a weight-mediated stabilization
hypothesis, remains confounded with function, and was not separated by the tested tuning grid. That
is a bounded observation about *this instrument*.
:::

1. **Seed counts are modest** (3–10 across these probes). Most sharply, the clean tracking
   $m$-vs-noise range ($0.62 \to 0.98$) is a **single-seed** read; the 5-seed run compresses it to
   $0.89 \to 0.98$ with a noisy frac30. The *direction* is robust; the exact sub-critical floor is not.
2. **The tracking `frac30` has ignition-mixture noise** — as the [tracking
   sweep](/experiments/tracking-param-sweep/) documents, performance over random inits is a
   chance-pinned failure mode plus a viable tail, so single-config frac30 numbers carry that variance.
3. **The current tuning grid did not answer the question for *this homeostatic node*.** That is not a
   statement about all possible interventions on this node or about criticality in general.
4. **The fixed-weight / evolved test is the open route.** A static-$\rho(W)$ reservoir (compartmental
   / CTRNN), evolved for the task at each $\lambda(W)$ level, is one promising design for making the
   network regime a more independent variable and testing whether it causally affects function.
