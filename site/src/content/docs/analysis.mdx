The analytic suite reads the *statistics of self-organised dynamics* at three scales.
These measures are **descriptors**—candidate dynamical explanations to read beside task
performance, not evidence of capacity by themselves. Every measure below carries its
equation, a one-line reading, and a **Source** callout to its Julia implementation. All
operate on channels recorded by the `Recorder` (off the compute hot path), so nothing here
perturbs a run.

## Before choosing a measure

Predeclare primary measures before confirmation; treat everything else as exploratory.
Specify the transient removal, window, threshold or binning choices, missing/dead-run
handling, estimator diagnostics, minimum data, multiple-comparison policy, and uncertainty
unit. Bootstrap the independent randomized run or block—not agents or ticks nested inside it.

| Family | Current role | Required discipline |
| --- | --- | --- |
| task/world metrics | operational performance and gates | task calibration and causal control |
| target/rate diagnostics | mechanism and failure diagnosis | compare against known component states |
| branching and avalanches | experimental criticality descriptors | fit diagnostics, surrogate/control, threshold and finite-size sensitivity |
| spectral radius | linear recurrent-matrix proxy | do not treat as general nonlinear stability |
| susceptibility/Fano/participation | experimental second-order descriptors | adequate population size and null |
| swarm order and contact graph | collective organization | torus-aware geometry and independent worlds |
| transfer entropy | experimental directional association | stationarity, bias, surrogates, multiplicity |

A nonsignificant contrast can mean low power, large uncertainty, or a small effect. It does
not establish “no effect”; that claim needs a declared equivalence margin and an equivalence
procedure. See [Design a study](/core/design-study/).

For a concrete, runnable end-to-end example that collects these measures at both scales
from a single forage rollout — per-agent branching, node/agent susceptibility,

## Criticality (node scale)

### Branching ratio — the MR estimator

For a population-activity series $A(t)$, the branching ratio $m$ is how much activity
one tick begets the next. The naive slope $\hat m = \sum A_t A_{t+1} / \sum A_t^2$ is
**biased when only a subset or noisy projection of the underlying process is observed**
(Wilting & Priesemann 2018). Reducing a fully observed population to a scalar total is not
by itself proof that the estimator's subsampling assumptions hold. The
**multistep-regression** estimator is more robust under its model assumptions: for lags
$k = 1\dots k_{\max}$ take the (intercept-corrected) slope $r_k$ of $A(t{+}k)$ on $A(t)$,
then fit

$$
r_k = b\,m^{k} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \log r_k = \log b + k\log m,
$$

and read $m$ off the exponential decay — which is invariant to the subsampling that
distorts the single-lag slope.

<p class="bl-source">Source: <code>src/analysis/Branching.jl</code> — <code>branching_ratio</code> (per-tick + legacy σ), <code>branching_ratio_mr</code>.</p>

:::caution
The Falandays homeostat regulates the population rate directly, so $m \approx 1$ can be
*rate-pinned* rather than emergent — read it alongside the spectral radius (below).
:::

### Neuronal avalanches

Threshold $A(t)$ into supra-threshold excursions; each avalanche has a **size** $S$
(summed activity) and **duration** $D$. Near criticality both are power laws,
$P(S)\sim S^{-\tau}$ and $P(D)\sim D^{-\alpha}$, tied by the **crackling-noise scaling
relation**

$$
\frac{\alpha-1}{\tau-1} \;=\; \gamma, \qquad \langle S\rangle(D)\sim D^{\gamma}.
$$

Agreement of the three exponents is still insufficient on its own for a criticality claim.
Require many events, threshold and bin sensitivity, competing-distribution checks, and
finite-size evidence; otherwise report the fitted avalanche descriptors only.

<p class="bl-source">Source: <code>src/analysis/Avalanches.jl</code> — <code>avalanches</code> (τ, α, γ_fit vs γ_pred).</p>

### Spectral radius

The dominant magnitude of the learned recurrent matrix, $\rho(W) = \max_i |\lambda_i(W)|$,
is a **linear proxy** that complements branching and avalanche reads. In a nonlinear,
threshold-reset, plastic spiking system it is not a general stability criterion. It is
tracked over the rollout and per agent.

<p class="bl-source">Source: <code>src/analysis/Spectral.jl</code>.</p>

### Per-node distance-to-target

Each node has a homeostatic target $T_i$; its instantaneous strain is $|a_i - T_i|$.
The across-node distribution is an "internal effort map" — which nodes are working,
which are settled.

## Cross-level — the same measure at two scales

One experimental programme supported by the platform measures the *same* signature at the **node** scale and
the **agent** scale, and ask where each sits relative to its *own* critical point — because
[information transmission is expected to peak near criticality](/notes/criticality-and-information/),
so locating the transition at each scale is the prerequisite for everything downstream.
Every criticality estimator takes a `level`:

- `:node` — each agent's own reservoir, as its per-agent population rate (fit per agent, then averaged — *not* node-to-node despite the name),
- `:agent` — the **per-tick count of agents undergoing a turn event** ($|\Delta\theta| > \theta_{\text{thr}}$, default $\pi/12$) — a genuine agent-scale cascade, *not* the pooled node rate rescaled,
- `:pooled` — all nodes of all agents in one series (the legacy default; it mixes distinct reservoirs, so read it as a population summary, not "the reservoir's" dynamics).

Not every measure is comparable across levels — this matters, because plotting an
incomparable pair against each other is a real inferential error. **Susceptibility** is the
natural candidate pair: node ($\chi = N\,\mathrm{Var}[\text{synchrony}]$) and agent
($\chi = n\,\mathrm{Var}[\text{polarization}]$) are formally the same quantity. In current
**unpromoted project observations**, neither agent susceptibility nor agent branching cleared
the circular-shift null; agent branching also appeared dominated by within-agent maneuvers.
These are working constraints, not promoted cross-level results. Node-scale measures stand on
their own; cross-level pairing remains an open, actively-nulled question. When a pair does survive,
compare its *peak positions* as a control parameter is swept, not raw magnitudes (the
prefactors $N\approx100$ vs $n\approx6$ differ by an order of magnitude).

<p class="bl-source">Source: <code>src/analysis/ActivityLevels.jl</code>; consumed by Branching / Avalanches / SecondOrder.</p>

### Second-order signatures (both levels)

$$
\chi = N\,\mathrm{Var}[\phi], \qquad
F = \frac{\mathrm{Var}[A]}{\langle A\rangle}, \qquad
\mathrm{PR} = \frac{(\sum_i \lambda_i)^2}{\sum_i \lambda_i^2}
$$

**Susceptibility** $\chi$ (order parameter $\phi$ = synchrony at node scale, polarization
at agent scale), the **Fano factor** $F$, and the **participation ratio** PR (effective
dimensionality of the activity covariance). A criticality hypothesis predicts these peak
together across levels as a control parameter is swept.

<p class="bl-source">Source: <code>src/analysis/SecondOrder.jl</code>.</p>

### Null test — `crossshift_null`

Before a cross-scale coupling number is trusted, run it through the circular-shift null.
Each surrogate independently circular-shifts every agent's recorded time series, so each
agent's *own* dynamics are preserved while the *inter-agent timing* is destroyed. The
measure is recomputed on the surrogate ensemble; comparing the real value against the
null distribution separates genuine cross-agent coupling from what any independent set of
single-agent series would produce by chance. Entity-aligned channels are shifted by stable
`EntityID`, not vector position. Derived/event channels are removed and static object
snapshots are retained; an unknown non-entity channel is an error by default rather than
being left deceptively aligned.

The result retains `real`, `null_mean`, `null_std`, and `ratio`, and also reports the full
`null_values`, `pvalue`, `alternative`, `n_requested`, and effective `n_valid`. The `ratio` is
descriptive: a value near $1$ does not establish equivalence or absence of coupling. Interpret
the declared-tail Monte Carlo `pvalue` against the full null distribution and finite
`n_valid`. Because circular shifts disrupt both inter-agent coupling and common-source timing,
pair this null with a causal condition appropriate to the claim—for example, vision-on versus
vision-off—rather than reading the raw ratio alone.

<p class="bl-source">Source: <code>src/analysis/NullTest.jl</code> — <code>crossshift_null</code>.</p>

## Collective (agent scale)

These are **descriptors (qualities), not competence scores** — they measure *how* a swarm is
organised, read alongside a task's capacity-indicator, never as a competence measure in their
own right (see [Scoring](/scoring/)).

- **Polarization** $\;P = \big|\frac{1}{N}\sum_i e^{i\theta_i}\big|$ — heading alignment, $0$ disordered → $1$ aligned.
- **Milling** — rotational order about the torus-aware circular centroid.
- **Cohesion** — mean nearest-neighbour + pairwise distance (periodic-boundary aware).
- **Swarm regime** — a discrete label (polarized, milling, swarming, or static) from $P$, milling, and speed.
- **Correlation length** — the distance at which the velocity-*fluctuation* correlation crosses zero (Cavagna et al. 2010 form: subtract the global mean velocity, normalize once globally).
- **Contact-graph clusters** — build the per-tick within-vision-radius contact graph and summarize its connected components: number of components, largest-component fraction, and mean component size (torus-aware). A graph-level readout of how the swarm fragments and coalesces (surfaced in sweeps as the `contact_clusters` measure).

<p class="bl-source">Source: <code>src/world/Metrics.jl</code> (polarization, milling, cohesion) and <code>src/analysis/SwarmAnalysis.jl</code> (<code>swarm_regime</code>, <code>correlation_length</code>, <code>contact_graph_clusters</code>).</p>

:::caution[Read these at the swept sample size]
Several estimators return a *confident number* rather than `NaN` when they are under-powered, so
read them against the actual run size. At `n_agents = 6` the **correlation length**, **agent-scale
susceptibility**, and **agent participation ratio** are severely under-sampled (6 points, 15
pairs) — time-pooling over a long rollout hides the noise but does not remove it; treat them as
uninterpretable below ~20–30 agents. **Branching (MR)** should be read with its per-agent
distribution and fit quality, not the bare mean — a low-$R^2$ exponential fit still returns a
finite $m$. **Avalanche** exponents need many events (tens to hundreds); a fit from a handful is
meaningless. And any *cross-agent* claim should clear the circular-shift null (`crossshift_null`,
`NullTest.jl`) before it is trusted — the null strips both real coupling and common-source drive,
so the coupling readout is the **vision-on minus vision-off difference**.
:::

## Information flow

**Transfer entropy** — directional, order-1 discrete plug-in estimator:

$$
\mathrm{TE}_{X\to Y} = \sum p(y_{t+1}, y_t, x_t)\,\log_2\frac{p(y_{t+1}\mid y_t, x_t)}{p(y_{t+1}\mid y_t)} .
$$

Two variants: node-to-node **within a reservoir** (spike trains), and agent-to-agent
(binarised heading change) — the direct measure for the social-foraging "do they use
each other as a guide?" question. *Experimental*: the plug-in estimate is biased at finite
samples. Check stationarity, compare against appropriate time-shift surrogates, report sample
counts, and correct across tested pairs. A KSG upgrade remains future work.
