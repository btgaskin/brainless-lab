## The setup

A 2D Ising system is driven by a **time-varying input** — an input spin flipped by a random telegraph
process with correlation time $\tau_s$ — and an output spin is read a distance $d$ away. This adds
what the static critical-flock picture lacks: a controllable *input timescale* and a clean separation
of two information measures.

## Two information measures

$$
I_{\text{inst}}(S;X) = H(S) - H(S\mid X), \qquad
I_R = \lim_{L\to\infty}\frac{I(S^L; X^L)}{L}.
$$

- **Instantaneous mutual information** $I_{\text{inst}}$ — the *accuracy* of the input→output map.
- **Information transmission rate** $I_R$ — accuracy *and speed*, folding in trajectory
  autocorrelation; with no output→input feedback it equals the **multi-step transfer entropy**.

They behave differently: $I_{\text{inst}}$ rises monotonically with $\tau_s$ to a plateau, whereas
$I_R$ has an **optimal finite $\tau_s$** — a trade-off between sending independent messages often,
responding fast, and responding reliably.

## The sharp results

- The transmission-rate optimum is **near but not *at*** criticality — on the *disordered* side (above
  $T_c$), because the response time diverges as the critical point is approached.
- The optimum **moves toward** the critical point as the transmission **distance $d$ grows**.
- It **moves away** as the **system size grows** at fixed $d$.

The governing trade-off is between the system's **response time** $\tau_r$ (the relaxation time of
spontaneous fluctuations) and the **input time** $\tau_s$ — a refinement of the "peak at criticality"
picture into "peak near criticality, set by matched timescales."
