## The question

Many biological systems self-organise near criticality (starling flocks, signal percolation in
bacterial communities, neural networks), where they map a **time-varying input** onto an output.
Earlier Ising studies showed mutual information / transfer entropy peaking at (or just above) $T_c$ —
but only between spins in **thermodynamic equilibrium**, not for the processing of time-varying signals
that drive the system *out* of equilibrium. This Letter asks how information flow depends on three
things left open: the **dynamics of the input**, the **distance** over which it must travel, and the
**distance to the critical point**.

## The setup

A 2D Ising system ($N\times N$, periodic boundaries, coupling $J$; $k_B T_c/J = 2.269$) under
discrete-time Glauber dynamics. One **input spin** $S$ is flipped by a stationary random-telegraph
process with timescale $\tau_s$, regulated *independently* of the remaining spins (whose dynamics are
set by the temperature $T$). This drives the system to a **stationary non-equilibrium steady state**.
An **output spin** $X$ sits a distance $d$ away along the diagonal. Because information propagates
through the intermediate spins, the output is strongly **non-Markovian**, so the full input/output
*history* matters.

## Two measures

- **Instantaneous mutual information** — the *accuracy* of the map at a single instant:
  $$
  I_{\text{inst}}(S;X) = H(S) - H(S\mid X),
  $$
  with $2^{I_{\text{inst}}}$ the number of reliably distinguishable input→output mappings.
- **Information transmission rate** — accuracy *and* speed, folding in the signals' autocorrelations:
  $$
  I_R = \lim_{L\to\infty}\frac{I(S^L; X^L)}{L},
  $$
  the rate at which the mutual information between input/output *trajectories* $S^L, X^L$ grows. With no
  output→input feedback, $I_R$ equals the **multi-step transfer entropy**. It requires trajectory
  lengths $L > \tau_s, \tau_r$, and is computed via a sampling-interval extrapolation with the Nemenman
  Bayesian entropy estimator.
- The **response time** $\tau_r$ is the relaxation time of spontaneous fluctuations in the *undriven*
  system (from the two-point time correlation), set by $T$ — and it **diverges near $T_c$**.

## Result 1 — accuracy has an optimal temperature, but is monotonic in $\tau_s$

$I_{\text{inst}}$ rises monotonically with $\tau_s$ to a plateau equal to the **static** mutual
information (input held fixed), and that plateau rises as $T$ falls (less thermal noise). But for short
$\tau_s \sim \tau_r$ it is **non-monotonic in $T$**: there is an optimal $T_{\text{opt}}(\tau_s)$ from a
trade-off — higher $T$ adds thermal noise (lowers $I_{\text{inst}}$) but shortens $\tau_r$, letting the
output track the input better (raises it). $T_{\text{opt}}$ decreases as $\tau_s$ grows.

## Result 2 — the rate is optimized *near, not at*, criticality

Unlike the accuracy, the **rate** $I_R$ has an **optimal finite input timescale** $\tau_s^{\text{opt}}$:
too fast and the output cannot keep up ($H(S^L\mid X^L)$ rises); too slow and time is wasted between
changes ($H(S^L)$ falls). Optimising over $\tau_s$, the peak rate $I_R^{\max}$ *itself* has an
**optimal temperature — and it lies above $T_c$**, because the response time diverges as $T\to T_c$,
throttling the bits sent per unit time. This is the headline: the transmission *rate* peaks close to,
but not at, the critical point (on the disordered side).

## Distance and size dependence

- As the transmission **distance $d$ grows**, $I_R^{\max}$ falls (weaker long-range correlations) and
  the optimal temperature **moves toward $T_c$** — a longer correlation length is needed to span $d$,
  which means sitting closer to criticality.
- At fixed $d$, increasing **system size** moves the optimum **away from $T_c$** (larger systems have a
  longer near-critical response time — up to 6× from $5\times5$ to $10\times10$ — which must be
  mitigated).
- Scaling $d$ *with* size (the RG-relevant finite-size question): $T_{\text{opt}} \approx 2.53$
  $(d{=}2,N{=}5) \to 2.44$ $(d{=}4,N{=}10) \to 2.38$ $(d{=}6,N{=}15)$ — hinting $T_{\text{opt}}\to T_c$
  in the thermodynamic limit.

## The trade-off, and the biological reading

$I_R$ reflects a three-way trade-off: maximise the **frequency** of independent input messages, respond
**fast** to changes, and respond **reliably**. Both $\tau_s$ and $T$ tune it, yielding an optimum
near-but-above criticality. Biologically: raising the **interaction strength** between components
(proteins in a signalling network, cells in a community, birds in a flock) increases reliability but
also the response time — so there is an **optimal coupling strength** that maximises $I_R$, a candidate
reason systems tune near a critical point. Because $\tau_r$ is dimension-independent while correlations
decay faster with distance in higher dimensions, they conjecture the optimum sits **closer to
criticality in higher-dimensional systems**.
