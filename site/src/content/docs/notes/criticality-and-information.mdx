## The principle

Many biological collectives appear to sit close to a **critical point** — the boundary between an
ordered and a disordered phase — from starling flocks to bacterial biofilms to cortical networks
(Mora & Bialek 2011; Chialvo 2010). The recurring conjecture is not that criticality *is*
computation, but that it is the regime in which **information transmission is optimised**: near the
transition, correlations reach across the whole system, so a perturbation felt by a few units can
be read out by all of them.

For BrainlessLab the useful way to say this is in the vocabulary the rest of these docs are built
on — **cue → signal**. A *cue* is structure an agent reads directly from the world; a *signal* is
something one agent produces that another acts on. Criticality is the regime in which **a cue held
by a few becomes a signal the collective acts on**: the lookout that sees the resource, the sentinel
that sees the predator, the informed minority (Couzin 2007; Buhl et al. 2006). The whole programme
here is to measure, at two nested scales, whether that cue-to-signal conversion is in fact best
near the critical point.

Two scales, because BrainlessLab contains two nested near-critical systems:

- the **reservoir** — the untrained nodes inside one agent, transducing its percepts;
- the **ensemble** — the swarm of agents, transducing the environment collectively.

The novel question the two-scale design exists to ask is whether the reservoir's proximity to
criticality *gates* the agent's contribution to transmission at the swarm scale — a mechanistic
chain, not a coincidence of two exponents.

## Mechanism I — collapses and correlation length (Vanni, Lukovic & Grigolini 2011)

*[Criticality and Transmission of Information in a Swarm of Cooperative Units](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevLett.107.078103),
Phys. Rev. Lett. **107**, 078103.*

Vanni et al. locate swarm "intelligence" in the **joint** action of two ingredients.

**Organizational collapses.** The global alignment $\Phi(t)$ intermittently drops below a threshold;
in that instant birds recover independence and are free to pick a new direction — the window in
which a small informed subset can steer the whole. The distribution of waiting times between
consecutive collapses is scale-free at criticality,

$$
\psi(\tau) \sim \tau^{-\mu}, \qquad \mu \approx 1.35,
$$

which they call **temporal complexity**. The mean inter-collapse time $\langle\tau\rangle$ stays
finite for $\mu > 2$ but *diverges* for $\mu < 2$ (the non-ergodic, ideal-complexity condition).

**Correlation length as large as the flock.** At criticality the velocity-fluctuation correlation
length $\xi$ grows with the system size $L$ ($\xi \sim L$), violating locality: the informed
minority's choice is felt everywhere.

**Transmission.** A lookout subset carries an environmental message $t(n) \in \{-1,+1\}$; a distant
unit's time-averaged, binarised signal $r(n)$ is correlated with it,

$$
C = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{n=1}^{B} r(n)\,t(n),
$$

and $C$ **peaks at criticality** (a bell curve in the control parameter). Sub-critical: the swarm
ignores the lookouts. Super-critical: the swarm is too rigid to re-synchronise. The transmission
delay is set by $\langle\tau\rangle$, so larger swarms transmit more slowly.

## Mechanism II — the near-but-not-at refinement (Meijers, Ito & ten Wolde 2021)

*[Behavior of information flow near criticality](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevE.103.L010102),
Phys. Rev. E **103**, L010102.*

Meijers et al. drive a 2D Ising system with a **time-varying input** — an input spin flipped by a
random telegraph process with correlation time $\tau_s$ — and read an output spin a distance $d$
away. This adds what Vanni's static picture lacks: a controllable *input timescale*, and a clean
separation of two information measures.

$$
I_{\text{inst}}(S;X) = H(S) - H(S\mid X), \qquad
I_R = \lim_{L\to\infty}\frac{I(S^L; X^L)}{L}.
$$

**Instantaneous mutual information** $I_{\text{inst}}$ measures the *accuracy* of the input→output
map. **Information transmission rate** $I_R$ measures accuracy *and speed*, folding in the
autocorrelation of the trajectories; with no output→input feedback it equals the **multi-step
transfer entropy**. The two behave differently: $I_{\text{inst}}$ rises monotonically with $\tau_s$
to a plateau, whereas $I_R$ has an **optimal finite $\tau_s$** — a trade-off between sending
independent messages often, responding fast, and responding reliably.

The sharp results, all falsifiable predictions for us:

- the optimum is **near but not *at*** criticality — on the *disordered* side (above $T_c$),
  because response time diverges as the critical point is approached;
- the optimum **moves toward** the critical point as the transmission **distance $d$ grows**;
- it **moves away** as the **system size grows** at fixed $d$.

The relevant trade-off variable is the **response time** $\tau_r$ — the relaxation time of
spontaneous fluctuations — playing against the input time $\tau_s$.

:::tip[The synthesis these two suggest together]
Vanni fixes the swarm's transmission delay at $\langle\tau\rangle$ (the inter-collapse time);
Meijers says there is an optimal input timescale $\tau_s^{\text{opt}}$. Put together they predict
**$\tau_s^{\text{opt}} \approx \langle\tau\rangle$** — the collective transmits best when the world
changes on the timescale of its own organizational collapses. This is *our* conjecture, not a claim
from either paper, and it is the kind of thing the two-scale harness is built to test.
:::

The criticality-*locating* toolkit already exists and is level-aware (`:node` / `:agent`); the
information-*rate* and *temporal-complexity* pieces are the targets.

:::caution[What is a *trustworthy* cross-scale quantity — and what is not]
Not every measure survives being read across scales. On foraging, agent-scale **branching ratio**
fails the circular-shift null (`crossshift_null`, `NullTest.jl`): its temporal autocorrelation comes
from *within-agent* maneuvers, not agent-to-agent propagation, and the shared source drive inflates
it. **Susceptibility** — synchrony at node scale, polarization at agent scale — is structurally
cross-agent and *passes*. So susceptibility is the natural same-measure-at-two-scales quantity;
agent-branching is kept only as a labelled null-failing negative control. Any new cross-agent
measure must clear the null before it is trusted, and the real coupling readout is the
**vision-on minus vision-off difference** — the null removes both real coupling and common-source
correlation, so only that difference isolates true transmission.
:::

## Future direction — the transmission task

None of the above requires a new task; the criticality and (endogenous) information-flow measures
run on ordinary rollouts once the collective actually self-organises. The *controlled transmission*
experiment — the direct test of "cue held by a few → signal acted on by all" — does need a small
mechanism that does not exist yet:

- **A lookout subset.** Today source vision is uniform and always-on; `conspecific_vision` blinds
  agents to *each other*, never to the source. A per-agent source-vision flag would let a subset
  read the resource cue while the rest are blind and can only receive it socially — through the
  existing **acoustic channel** (distance-attenuated, one-tick lag), which is the natural signal
  substrate. This is the cue→signal conversion made literal.
- **A time-varying source** (optional, and the more speculative half). A stationary beacon offers a
  message only during the approach; a **slowly drifting source** gives a continuous, stationary
  stream to transmit and a $\tau_s$ set by drift speed — the ecologically honest way to get the
  Meijers input-timescale knob without an artificial telegraph.

Both are downstream of a sweep that reliably produces foraging / swarming / shoaling in the first
place. They are recorded here so the design does not have to be re-derived when that day comes.
