## The claim

Swarm "intelligence" — a small informed subset (lookouts) steering the whole flock toward a resource
or away from a predator — is located at a **critical point**, and emerges from the *joint* action of
two ingredients.

## Ingredient 1 — organizational collapses (temporal complexity)

The global alignment order parameter $\Phi(t)$ (the swarm's mean flight direction) intermittently
drops below a threshold; in that instant the birds recover independence and are free to pick a new
direction — the window in which a small informed subset can steer the whole. At criticality the
distribution of waiting times between consecutive collapses is **scale-free**:

$$
\psi(\tau) \sim \tau^{-\mu}, \qquad \mu \approx 1.35,
$$

which they call **temporal complexity**. The mean inter-collapse time $\langle\tau\rangle$ stays finite
for $\mu > 2$ but *diverges* for $\mu < 2$ (the non-ergodic, ideal-complexity condition). A finite
swarm truncates the power law exponentially at long times, at a cutoff that grows with size.

## Ingredient 2 — correlation length as large as the flock

At criticality the velocity-fluctuation correlation length $\xi$ grows with the system size $L$
($\xi \sim L$), violating locality: the informed minority's choice is felt everywhere, and because
$\langle\tau\rangle \sim L$ too, larger swarms transmit **more slowly**.

## Transmission peaks at criticality

A lookout subset carries an environmental message $t(n) \in \{-1,+1\}$; a distant unit's
time-averaged, binarised signal $r(n)$ is correlated with it,

$$
C = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{n=1}^{B} r(n)\,t(n),
$$

and $C$ **peaks at criticality** — a bell curve in the control parameter. Sub-critically the swarm
ignores the lookouts (too independent); super-critically it is too rigid to re-synchronise. The
transmission delay is set by $\langle\tau\rangle$. The decision-making lattice model reproduces the
same picture, so the result is not special to the Vicsek dynamics.
