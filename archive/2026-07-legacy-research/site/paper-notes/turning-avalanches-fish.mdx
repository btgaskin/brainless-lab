## The study

A large-statistics empirical analysis of spontaneous **turning avalanches** — large heading changes
propagating across a group — in freely swimming schools (black neon tetra, $N=8,16,32,50$; $>10^4$
avalanche events). It applies the *full* condensed-matter/seismology avalanche toolkit and finds
**scale-free behaviour** — power laws, an exponent scaling relation, and data collapses — which it is
careful to call a **necessary (not sufficient)** condition for criticality.

## Definition and observables

An individual's **turning rate** $\omega = |\mathbf v\times\mathbf a|/v^2$ (angular-velocity magnitude);
above a threshold $\omega_{\text{th}}$ a fish is "active." An **avalanche** is a run of consecutive frames
with $\ge1$ active fish, characterised by **duration** $T$, **size** $S=\sum_t n_t$, and **interevent
time** $t_i$; the **activity rate** $r$ is the fraction of frames inside an avalanche.

## Distributions, scaling relations, collapses

At $\omega_{\text{th}}=0.1$: $P(T)\sim T^{-\alpha}$ ($\alpha\approx2.4$), $P(S)\sim S^{-\tau}$
($\tau\approx1.97$), average size $\langle S\rangle_T\sim T^{m}$ with $m\approx1.41$ — obeying the
**crackling-noise scaling relation**

$$
m = \frac{\alpha-1}{\tau-1},
$$

and interevent times $P(t_i)\sim t_i^{-\gamma}$ ($\gamma\approx1.62$). Distributions for different $N$
collapse (bar the finite-size tails). At **fixed activity rate**, duration and interevent-time
distributions collapse across $N$; the interevent times obey the SOC form
$P(t_i)=\tfrac1{\langle t_i\rangle}\Psi(t_i/\langle t_i\rangle)$; and the **avalanche shape** obeys
$\langle n_t\rangle = T^{m-1}\,\mathcal F(t/T)$ — a universal temporal profile.

## The key methodological point

The authors are explicit that **power laws alone don't demonstrate criticality** (other mechanisms make
them); the **tighter** evidence is the **data collapses and the relation between exponents**
($m=(\alpha-1)/(\tau-1)$, the shape collapse), which indicate *quantitatively universal* avalanche dynamics
across scales. With no externally tuned parameter, a near-critical school would be an instance of
**self-organized criticality**.

## Boundaries, function, correlations

- **Dragon kings.** The distribution tails carry over-represented extreme events generated by a *different*
  mechanism — **tank-wall** interactions (large avalanches cluster at corners). Restricting to the tank
  centre removes them (statistical test: $p<10^{-15}$ overall vs. $p=0.1$ centred) and extends the clean
  power law to ~2 decades.
- **Function.** Large avalanches occur at the **speed minima** of the burst-and-coast cycle, temporarily
  *reduce* polarization and then *recover* it — i.e. they are **collective decision-making** about a new
  heading (U-turns are a special case). **Boundary individuals** are the frequent **initiators** (higher
  social influence / more risk-exposed).
- **Aftershocks.** Using seismology's Gutenberg–Richter ($P(m)\sim e^{-bm}$, $m=\ln S$, $b=\tau-1$) and an
  **Omori law** $P(t)=K/(t+c)^p$, correlated aftershocks cluster below a **half-turn timescale**
  (~250 frames), with $p\approx2.2$ — a *faster* decay than earthquakes, and no collective memory beyond
  that timescale.

## Why it's collected here

This is, almost exactly, the **empirical protocol for the reorientation-cascade experiment on our own
next-set list** — turning avalanches, defined by a heading-change threshold, analysed with $P(S)\sim S^{-\tau}$,
$P(T)\sim T^{-\alpha}$, the $m=(\alpha-1)/(\tau-1)$ scaling relation, data collapse and avalanche-shape
collapse. Three things to carry straight over: the exponents to compare against ($\tau\approx1.5$–2,
$\alpha\approx2$); the insistence that **collapses + exponent relations, not a bare power law**, are the
real test (the Muñoz/Watkins caution, operationalised); and the **dragon-king / boundary** lens — our torus
has no walls, but the *source* is a boundary-like attractor, so the same "are the extreme events a
different mechanism?" question applies. It also makes **turning = collective decision-making**, feeding the
collective-decision-task idea in our plan.
