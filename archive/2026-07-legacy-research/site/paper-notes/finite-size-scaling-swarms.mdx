## The gap it fills

Earlier claims that biological groups sit near criticality had two weaknesses: the control parameter was
always **inferred through a model** (prone to undersampling), and off-equilibrium conservation laws can
produce long-range correlations *generically* ("generic scale invariance") without any tuning. Worse, the
critical point is sharp only in the thermodynamic limit ŌĆö at finite $N$ the sole remnant is a
**susceptibility peak whose position depends on $N$**. So a single bulk critical value is *not* critical
across sizes (a tiny Ising model at bulk $T_c$ is deeply magnetised). The two missing pieces this paper
supplies: (i) a **direct measurement** of the control parameter, and (ii) evidence that across sizes the
control parameter varies **with $N$** to stay near the susceptibility maximum. The lesson: it is the
**pair $(x, N)$** that must sit in the scaling region, not merely proximity to $x_c$.

## System and observables

Wild midge swarms (Diptera) reconstructed in 3D, $N \approx 100$ŌĆō$600$, in the **disordered phase** (low
polarization $\Phi \approx 0.2$) yet strongly correlated. From velocity fluctuations $\delta\vec\varphi$
(with instantaneous global translation, rotation *and* dilatation subtracted ŌĆö a non-equilibrium system
has no usable time average), they build the connected correlation $C(r)$, take the correlation length
$\xi \sim r_0$ (its first zero, far larger than the nearest-neighbour distance), and the **susceptibility**
$\chi$ = integrated correlation up to $r_0$ (ŌłØ number of correlated individuals). Natural swarms reach
$\chi$ up to ~5.6 vs. ~0.1 for a non-interacting system (50├Ś more correlated). The control parameter is
the nearest-neighbour distance rescaled by the interaction range, $x = r_1/\lambda \approx r_1/l$ (the
acoustic interaction range scales with body length $l$).

## The Vicsek yardstick and finite-size scaling

The 3D Vicsek model has a density-driven order/disorder transition at $x_c$. Its bulk transition is
first-order, but only above a huge crossover size ($N^\star\sim10^6$); **below** it ŌĆö the swarm-relevant
regime ŌĆö a pseudo-second-order phenomenology holds and standard finite-size scaling applies:

$$
x_{\max}(N) = x_c + N^{-1/3\nu}, \qquad y = (x - x_c)\,N^{1/3\nu},
$$
$$
\chi \sim N^{\gamma/3\nu}\,f(y), \qquad \xi \sim L\,g(y).
$$

Two paths matter (map in the paper's Fig. 2a): at **fixed $x$**, growing $N$ makes $\chi$ and $\xi$
**saturate** (you fall away from the peak); along **constant $y$**, $\chi \sim N^{\gamma/3\nu}$ and
$\xi \sim L$ stay **scale-free** (you track the peak). 3D-Vicsek fit: $\nu\approx0.75$, $\gamma\approx1.6$,
$x_c\approx0.42$ (near 3D Heisenberg).

## The result

Natural swarms show **no saturation** of $\chi$ or $\xi$ with size ŌĆö $\chi$ scales with $N$ and $\xi$ with
$L$ up to the largest swarms. That places them on the **constant-$y$ path**: as $N$ grows, $x$ *decreases*
(following the susceptibility peak while staying on the disordered side), evidenced by a direct
$x$ŌĆō$N$ correlation. So $(x,N)$ lives in the near-critical region, with $\chi \sim (x-x_c)^{-\gamma}$. The
new content over prior work: the control parameter is **measured, not inferred**, and what is seen is not
a generic vicinity but a **mutual adjustment of $x$ and $N$** that keeps correlations scale-free ŌĆö which
rules out generic scale invariance. (The fitted swarm exponents are not conclusive given the limited size
span, but the *scaling* result is fit-independent.)

## Two readings of near-criticality

- **Adjust $x$ given $N$** ŌĆö tune the control parameter toward $x_{\max}(N)$; requires individuals to
  assess global correlation via a *local proxy* and readjust as $N$ changes.
- **Grow $N$ given $x$** ŌĆö a swarm aggregates up to a maximum sustainable size $N_{\max}(x)$: below it the
  group stays scale-free-correlated ($\xi/L$ constant via the Goldstone mode of the ordered phase), beyond
  it correlation deteriorates. A mating-driven aggregation mechanism plausibly enforces such a ceiling ŌĆö
  and may explain why swarms stay disordered.

A caveat with reach: the second (aggregation) reading relies on **continuous** symmetry breaking
(Goldstone ŌåÆ divergent bulk $\xi$). For **discrete** symmetry (Ising-like, as in many neural models) it
fails, and an adaptive-$x$ mechanism seems required instead.
