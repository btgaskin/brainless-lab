## The result

For a network of coupled **excitable** nodes, criticality and maximal **dynamic range** are governed by
one quantity: the **largest eigenvalue $\lambda$ of the weighted adjacency matrix** (the spectral radius).
**$\lambda=1$ is the critical point with maximum dynamic range**, universally — for random, scale-free,
assortative, and degree-correlated topologies alike. This **generalizes** the earlier branching-ratio /
mean-degree criterion $\sigma=1$, which *fails* for heterogeneous degree distributions.

## Setup and the old criterion

In the Kinouchi–Copelli model, a resting node is excited by an active neighbour $j$ with probability
$A_{ij}$, or by external stimulus with probability $\eta$; the response $F$ is the time-averaged excited
fraction, and **dynamic range** $\Delta = 10\log_{10}(\eta_{\text{high}}/\eta_{\text{low}})$ measures the
span of stimuli that produce distinguishable responses. The prior theory said criticality occurs at
**branching ratio** $\sigma = \tfrac1N\sum_{ij}A_{ij} = \langle d\rangle = 1$ (one excitation begets one),
where avalanches are power-law — but this holds only for homogeneous (Erdős–Rényi) networks.

## The derivation

Linearising the excitation dynamics about the quiescent fixed point $p=0$ gives
$\lambda u_i = \sum_j u_j A_{ij}$ — so stability is set by the **largest eigenvalue** $\lambda$ ($\lambda<1$
quiescent, $\lambda>1$ active; Perron–Frobenius makes $\lambda$ real and positive). Near critical, the
excitation profile aligns with the **dominant eigenvector** $u$, and a weakly-nonlinear expansion (using
the left/right eigenvectors $u,v$) yields the response in terms of a few **spectral properties** of $A$,
e.g.

$$
F_{\eta\to0} = \frac{\lambda-1}{\lambda+\tfrac12\lambda^2}\,\frac{\langle uv\rangle\langle u\rangle}{\langle u^2 v\rangle}.
$$

The bridge to the old rule: $\lambda \approx \rho\,\langle d^{\text{in}}d^{\text{out}}\rangle/\langle d\rangle$
(with $\rho$ the assortativity), which reduces to $\lambda\approx\langle d\rangle$ only when the network is
homogeneous and uncorrelated. Simulations across six network families confirm the transition sits at
**$\lambda=1$, not $\langle d\rangle=1$** — including cases tuned through criticality by changing
*assortativity alone* at fixed mean degree.

## Homogeneity raises dynamic range

The peak dynamic range carries a topology term,
$\Lambda_{\max} = 10\log_{10}\!\frac{2}{3F_*^2} - 10\log_{10}\frac{\langle vu^2\rangle}{\langle v\rangle\langle u\rangle^2}$;
for an uncorrelated undirected network the second term is $\approx -10\log_{10}(\langle d^3\rangle/\langle d\rangle^3)$,
maximised when the degree is **uniform**. So **homogeneous topologies achieve higher dynamic range** than
heterogeneous (scale-free) ones — confirmed by scale-free nets with larger exponent $\gamma$ (more
homogeneous) reaching higher range. The authors close with the conjecture that if wide dynamic range is
adaptive, evolution may **homogenize** brain topology, and that real brains operate at $\lambda\approx1$.

## Why it's collected here

This is the most directly actionable paper for our **node/reservoir scale**. The controlling quantity is
literally the **spectral radius** — the `spectral_radius` measure already in the harness — and the paper
says $\lambda=1$ is the *correct, topology-general* critical point, of which the branching-ratio $\sigma=1$
(what our node-branching estimator targets) is only the homogeneous special case. That reframes our own
observations cleanly: in the N-sweep the spectral radius rising *through* the performance peak, and
$m_{\text{node}}\approx1$ under homeostasis, are two views of $\lambda\to1$; and the "**homogeneous
topology → higher dynamic range**" result is a concrete, testable prediction for the connectome variants
(link density, degree distribution) we've been sweeping. **Dynamic range** is also a candidate functional
readout — a principled, criticality-linked alternative to the raw forage score.
